Robot kit

When I was first learning to use programmable electronics I purchased one of the many 'Arduino starter kits', which contained the microcontroller and a variety of sensors, a display, and a servo. This was helpful to start, but it only got so far. I was able to put it together, but the instructions and videos only really told me how to put it together, not what I was doing or why it worked. Years later, I am now designing a started kit of my own which I hope will provied others with the tools and knowledge I had to scrounge for myself.

After doing some brainstorming I came up with the following things I wanted to have included in my kit, that aren't in many others:

  • Power - powering the microcontroller and other components from an external source.
  • Higher power actuators - not all things can be run directly off a microcontroller, some require controller modules.
  • ICs - using other ICs was a massive hurdle for me, but they have so many uses I wanted my users to be used to them.
  • Ohm's law - not a difficult concept but so important and users mightn't have known it before.
  • Bluetooth connectivity - in today's world interconnectivity is crucial, and it's never too early to start.
  • Screen - displaying things on a screen is essential in the modern age, and it will be a part of my kit.

On top of all this I wanted those using my kit to get an impressive and satisfying project at the end (rather than a series of random and disconnected demonstrations), so all the individual components would add together to one bigger robot.

I started with the individual components. The first I worked on was power management. For this I used two voltage regulators. A 5V one for the motors, and a 3.3V one for the microcontroller. This would allow me to power the circuit with a 4-6 AA batteries. The voltage regulators (and the calculations involved with them) will also give users an introduction into controlling power.

Next I moved to the motor driver IC. I was using a L293D IC which is a very commonly used IC (and is the basis of many 'plug and play' motor driver units). After soldering some jumper wires to the motors and hooking the Raspberry Pi Pico up the citcuit was ready to go, and worked well!

Some of the components of my robot. Raspberry Pi Pico (left). Voltage regulator circuits (centre). Motor driver IC (right).

Now I had a few of the components sorted it was time to lasercut a frame. By this point I already knew the bulk of the electronics would be contained on a breadboard, so I needed to make my frame around that. I also had to make room for the other sensors I had decided to put in: an ultrasonic distance sensor, and a servo with a colour sensor on it.

I drew up a frame on Adobe Illustrator, then sent it to my laser cutter.

A timelapse of my laser cutter cutting a prototype frame.

The holes when I made them were a little too large, so after gluing the pieces together I had a functional frame.

The frame while the glue was drying.

After the glue had dried I assembled the voltage regulators, motor driver, and microcontroller on the circuit, then inserted the motors and fed the wires through the frame back to the breadboard.

Adding bluetooth was a bit of a pain. Not on the electronics side, that was fairly simple as serial communication is a robust and much used system. The difficult bit was choosing which app to use. After downloading a dozen on both iPhones and Android apps, I can recommend 'Bluetooth for Arduino for iOS and 'Serial Bluetooth Terminal' for Android. The latter is the better app by far, but the former is usable too.

I couldn't wait to take my robot for a spin, so before I put the rest of the sensors in I wrote a quick program to test out its agility. I had previously pulled one of the motors apart (completely by accident) as I was fitting it into the robot, and though I put it together, I must have damaged it in the process - so one motor was very weak. But regardless, it worked.

Testing my robot's turning.

The next things I have to do is sort out the distance sensor, servo, and colour sensor, screen, and buttons, then write the program for it. Once the kit is complete I aim to film video tutorials explaining not only how they are put together but also why things work and how they are applicable - then sell them on Etsy.
